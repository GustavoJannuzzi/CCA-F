What Is Claude? -- The Foundation

Understand what Claude is, how it works, and the different ways you can interact with it. This module builds the conceptual foundation that everything else in the training program rests on.

⏱ ~45 min 📚 Beginner 🎓 Everyone 🎯 4 Interactive Exercises

1. The AI Landscape

What Are Large Language Models (LLMs)?

Large Language Models are neural networks trained on massive text datasets -- books, websites, code repositories, scientific papers, and more. At their core, they work by predicting the next token (word or word-piece) in a sequence. But this deceptively simple mechanism creates emergent capabilities that go far beyond mere text completion: reasoning, analysis, code generation, translation, and nuanced conversation.

A helpful analogy: think of autocomplete on your phone, but elevated by many orders of magnitude. Your phone's autocomplete predicts the next word based on shallow patterns. An LLM predicts the next token based on a deep understanding of context, logic, nuance, and even intent. The gap between the two is enormous -- like the difference between a calculator and a mathematician.

How Claude Differs from Other AI Assistants

Claude is built by Anthropic, a company founded with safety at its core. Several things set Claude apart:

  • Constitutional AI (CAI) -- Rather than relying solely on lists of rules, Claude is trained to follow high-level principles. It learns to be helpful, harmless, and honest through a process that embeds these values deeply into its behavior.
  • Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) -- Human evaluators rate Claude's responses, and the model is fine-tuned to align with human preferences for quality, accuracy, and safety.
  • Helpful, Harmless, and Honest -- This is Anthropic's guiding triad. Claude strives to provide genuinely useful answers, avoid causing harm, and be transparent about what it knows and does not know.
  • Exceptional at analysis, reasoning, coding, and nuanced writing -- Claude consistently excels in benchmarks for complex reasoning, code generation, and long-form writing tasks.

A Brief History of LLMs

Understanding where LLMs came from helps contextualize where they are going:

  • 2017: Transformers -- Google researchers published "Attention Is All You Need," introducing the transformer architecture. This replaced slower recurrent neural networks with a parallelizable self-attention mechanism, unlocking massive scale.
  • 2018-2020: Scaling Up -- GPT, BERT, GPT-2, and GPT-3 demonstrated that bigger models trained on more data produced qualitatively better results. Each generation showed new emergent capabilities.
  • 2021-2022: Alignment -- Researchers discovered that raw capability was not enough -- models needed to be aligned with human values. RLHF and Constitutional AI emerged as techniques to make models helpful, honest, and harmless.
  • 2023-Present: Agents -- LLMs evolved from text generators to autonomous agents that can use tools, plan multi-step actions, and interact with external systems. Claude Code is a prime example of this evolution.
ⓘ

Why safety matters for enterprise: When you deploy AI in a business context, you need a model that knows when to decline a request, explains its reasoning, and does not fabricate authoritative-sounding but incorrect answers. Claude's safety training directly addresses these enterprise concerns.

2. The Claude Model Family

Claude comes in three model tiers, each optimized for different tradeoffs between speed, cost, and capability. Choosing the right model for your task is one of the most impactful decisions you will make when working with Claude.

Haiku 4.5 -- Fast and Cost-Effective

Haiku is the speedster of the family. It responds in roughly 200 milliseconds for short queries and costs a fraction of what the larger models charge. It is purpose-built for tasks that are well-defined, repetitive, or high-volume.

  • Best for: Simple classification, routing, quick Q&A, high-volume tasks
  • Response time: ~200ms for short queries
  • Cost: Lowest tier -- ideal when you need to process thousands of items

Sonnet 4.6 -- The Balanced Workhorse

Sonnet hits the sweet spot between capability and cost. For most day-to-day tasks -- coding, analysis, writing, conversation -- Sonnet delivers excellent quality at a speed and price point that makes it the default choice for the majority of workflows.

  • Best for: Most tasks -- coding, analysis, writing, conversation
  • Excellent speed-to-quality ratio -- fast enough for interactive use, capable enough for complex work
  • Cost: Mid-tier, outstanding value for the capability level

Opus 4.6 -- Maximum Capability

Opus is Claude at its most powerful. When you face genuinely hard problems -- complex architecture decisions, subtle debugging, multi-layered research, or tasks that require holding many constraints in mind simultaneously -- Opus is the model to reach for.

  • Best for: Complex reasoning, architecture design, nuanced analysis, research
  • Deepest understanding -- the most thorough, considered responses
  • Cost: Highest, but worth it for problems where getting it right matters most

Practical Example: A Day of Model Choices

Imagine a typical day for a software engineering lead. Here is how they might use all three models across different tasks:

Morning: Triage # Haiku: Classify 200 overnight support tickets # Task: Simple categorization into bug/feature/question/billing # Why Haiku: High volume, simple classification, speed matters # Cost: ~$0.50 total for 200 tickets
Midday: Development # Sonnet: Help refactor the payment service # Task: Rewrite a module to use the new API, add tests # Why Sonnet: Good coding ability, fast iteration, reasonable cost # Cost: ~$2-5 for a multi-turn coding session
Afternoon: Architecture # Opus: Design the migration strategy from monolith to microservices # Task: Analyze dependencies, propose service boundaries, plan rollout # Why Opus: Multi-constraint reasoning, needs deepest analysis # Cost: ~$10-15 for a thorough architecture session
💡

Rule of thumb: Start with Sonnet. If the task is simple and you need speed or volume, drop to Haiku. If Sonnet's output is not quite deep or nuanced enough, escalate to Opus. Most people find that Sonnet handles 80% of their work perfectly well.

When to Upgrade Your Model Choice

How do you know when Sonnet is not enough and you should switch to Opus? Watch for these signs:

  • Sonnet's answer feels surface-level -- If you ask for an architecture review and get generic advice rather than specific insights about your codebase, Opus may find the deeper patterns.
  • The task has many interacting constraints -- Compliance requirements + performance targets + backward compatibility + team bandwidth = a problem that benefits from Opus's deeper reasoning.
  • You are debugging something subtle -- Race conditions, memory leaks, edge cases in distributed systems -- these require the kind of careful analysis where Opus shines.
  • The cost of getting it wrong is high -- For a production migration plan or a security audit, the extra cost of Opus is trivial compared to the cost of missing something important.

3. Three Surfaces, One Intelligence

Claude's intelligence is consistent across all interfaces -- the same models, the same training, the same capabilities. What changes is the surface: the interface optimized for different workflows. Think of it as the same engine under three different vehicle bodies, each designed for a different type of road.

🌐

Claude in the Browser

Chat-based interface at claude.ai that anyone can use. Create Projects for persistent context, use Artifacts for documents, code, and diagrams.

Best for: writing, analysis, research, brainstorming
💻

Claude Code (CLI)

Command-line AI pair programmer. Reads, writes, and edits code directly. Runs commands, manages git. Has 8 core tools: Read, Write, Edit, Bash, Glob, Grep, Agent, WebSearch.

Best for: development, code review, debugging, automation
👥

Claude Cowork

Shared team workspace for collaboration. Persistent team context, shared knowledge bases, governance and usage controls.

Best for: team workflows, enterprise, collaborative authoring

Claude in the Browser (claude.ai)

The browser interface is the most accessible way to interact with Claude. Open a browser, go to claude.ai, and start chatting. It is ideal for knowledge work -- writing, analysis, research, brainstorming, and creative tasks.

  • Projects -- Organize conversations with persistent context. Upload reference documents, set custom instructions, and maintain continuity across sessions.
  • Artifacts -- Claude can generate standalone documents, code files, and interactive Mermaid diagrams right in the conversation. Think of them as deliverables Claude creates alongside its explanations.
  • No installation required -- works in any modern browser.

Claude Code (CLI)

Claude Code runs in your terminal as a full-fledged autonomous agent. It does not just suggest code in a chat window -- it actually reads your files, writes edits, runs your test suite, manages git, and builds software. It reasons about your codebase and executes multi-step plans.

  • 8 core tools: Read, Write, Edit, Bash, Glob, Grep, Agent, WebSearch
  • Operates on your local filesystem with a permission system for safety
  • Ideal for software development, code review, debugging, and automation
  • Installs via npm install -g @anthropic-ai/claude-code
Example: Claude Code in Action # You type a natural language request: "Add a health check endpoint to our FastAPI backend that returns the server uptime and database connection status." # Claude Code autonomously: # 1. Searches for the FastAPI app definition (Grep) # 2. Reads the main app file (Read) # 3. Adds the new endpoint (Edit) # 4. Reads existing tests for patterns (Read) # 5. Creates a new test file (Write) # 6. Runs the test suite to verify (Bash) # 7. Reports success with a summary

Claude Cowork (Team Workspace)

Cowork brings Claude into a shared, collaborative environment designed for teams. It provides persistent context that the whole team can access, shared knowledge bases, and enterprise-grade governance controls.

  • Shared workspace -- Multiple team members interact with the same context
  • Governance -- Audit logs, content filtering, usage policies, role-based access
  • Enterprise adoption -- Designed for organizations scaling Claude across departments
ⓘ

Which surface should I use? Use the browser for general knowledge work. Use Claude Code for anything involving code on your machine. Use Cowork when your team needs shared context and governance. Many people use all three depending on the task at hand.

4. Understanding Tokens and Context

What Are Tokens?

Tokens are the fundamental unit of text that Claude processes. When you send a message, Claude does not see individual characters or even whole words -- it sees tokens, which are pieces of text that typically correspond to common word fragments.

  • Roughly 1 token = 4 characters = 0.75 words
  • "Hello, world!" = approximately 4 tokens
  • A typical English sentence = about 15-25 tokens
  • Common words like "the", "is", "and" are usually 1 token each
  • Longer or uncommon words may be split across multiple tokens

The Context Window

The context window is Claude's working memory -- everything Claude can consider at one time. It includes your system prompt, the entire conversation history, all tool results, and the current message.

  • Standard: 200,000 tokens (~150,000 words, or roughly 500 pages of text)
  • Opus extended: Up to 1,000,000 tokens (available for the Opus model)
  • The context window includes: system prompt + conversation history + tool results + current message
⚠

Context is finite. When the context window fills up, earlier parts of the conversation are compressed or lost. This means very long conversations gradually degrade as earlier context gets summarized. Managing context effectively is a key skill for power users.

Quick Token Reference

To build intuition, here is what different types of content typically cost in tokens:

Content Type Approximate Size Tokens % of 200K Window

A detailed prompt 1 paragraph ~100-200 0.1%

A long research paper ~30 pages ~15,000-20,000 10%

Input vs. Output Tokens

Token costs are split into two categories, and the distinction matters for budgeting:

  • Input tokens -- Everything you send to Claude: your prompts, uploaded files, system instructions, conversation history. These are the cheaper of the two categories.
  • Output tokens -- Everything Claude generates back: responses, code, explanations. These cost more than input tokens because generation requires more computation.
💡

Budget tip: Be specific in your prompts to get concise, targeted responses. A vague prompt like "tell me about Python" will generate a long, expensive response. A precise prompt like "explain Python's GIL in 3 sentences" produces a focused answer that costs a fraction of the tokens.

5. Safety and Responsible Use

Safety is not a bolt-on feature for Claude -- it is woven into the model's training from the ground up. Understanding how Claude approaches safety helps you work with it more effectively and set appropriate expectations.

Constitutional AI

Instead of maintaining an ever-growing list of specific prohibited behaviors, Anthropic trains Claude using Constitutional AI. Claude learns to follow principles -- be helpful, be harmless, be honest -- and applies those principles to novel situations it has never seen before. This makes Claude's safety behavior more robust and generalizable than rule-based systems.

Refusal Behavior

Claude will decline requests that would cause harm, but it does so transparently. Rather than silently ignoring a request or giving a vague non-answer, Claude explains why it is declining and often suggests alternative approaches that achieve the user's legitimate goal without the harmful elements.

System Prompts

System prompts allow organizations to constrain Claude's behavior for specific use cases. For example, a customer support deployment might instruct Claude to only answer questions about the company's products, while a code review tool might focus Claude on security and performance issues. System prompts shape behavior without retraining the model.

Enterprise Controls

  • Audit logs -- Track every interaction for compliance and review
  • Content filtering -- Additional guardrails for sensitive industries
  • Usage policies -- Set limits on who can use which models and how much
  • Role-based access -- Control which teams and individuals can access specific capabilities

Data Handling

By default, conversations with Claude are not used for training. Your data remains your data. For enterprise deployments, Anthropic offers additional data handling commitments and compliance certifications. This is a critical consideration for organizations working with sensitive information.

Best Practices for Responsible Use

Being an effective Claude user means understanding where AI excels and where human judgment remains essential:

  • Always verify critical outputs. If Claude generates a legal clause, a financial calculation, or a security configuration, have a domain expert review it before using it in production.
  • Do not share sensitive credentials. Avoid pasting API keys, passwords, or other secrets into conversations. Even though conversations are not used for training, this is good security hygiene.
  • Understand Claude's limitations. Claude's training data has a knowledge cutoff. For very recent events, breaking news, or rapidly evolving topics, verify information from authoritative sources.
  • Use system prompts for guardrails. When deploying Claude for a specific use case (customer support, code review, content creation), write a clear system prompt that defines acceptable behavior, output format, and scope limitations.
  • Iterate on safety. If Claude produces an unexpected or concerning output, report it and adjust your prompts. Safety is a collaborative process between the user and the model.
⚠

Your responsibility: While Claude has extensive safety training, it is not infallible. Always review Claude's outputs, especially for tasks involving sensitive data, legal content, medical information, or production deployments. Claude is a powerful tool, but the human in the loop remains essential.
