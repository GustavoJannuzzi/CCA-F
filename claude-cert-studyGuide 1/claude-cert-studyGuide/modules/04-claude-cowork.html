Claude Cowork -- Team AI Collaboration

Learn how Claude Cowork transforms AI from a personal tool into a team capability. Shared context, governance controls, and collaborative workflows -- all in one workspace.

⏱ ~30 min 📚 Beginner 🎓 Everyone 🎯 3 Interactive Exercises

1. What Is Claude Cowork?

Claude Cowork is Anthropic's collaborative AI workspace designed for teams and organizations. While Claude.ai in the browser is built for individual use and Claude Code is built for software engineers in the terminal, Cowork is purpose-built for team collaboration with shared context, governance, and enterprise-grade controls.

Think of it this way: if Claude.ai is your personal AI assistant and Claude Code is your AI pair programmer, then Claude Cowork is your team's shared AI colleague -- one that knows your company's standards, processes, and goals, and works consistently across every team member.

How Cowork Differs from Claude.ai (Browser)

The browser version of Claude is designed for individual productivity. You open it, type a question, get an answer, and move on. Every conversation is yours alone. Cowork takes a fundamentally different approach:

  • Shared context: Upload company documents, brand guidelines, and process documentation that every team member's conversations can reference.
  • Team management: Invite colleagues, assign roles, and control who can do what within the workspace.
  • Governance: Audit logs, usage policies, and compliance controls that satisfy enterprise security requirements.
  • Consistency: Custom instructions at the workspace level ensure everyone gets responses that follow your company's standards.

How Cowork Differs from Claude Code

Claude Code is a CLI-based agentic tool that reads files, writes code, and runs commands on your local machine. It is built specifically for software development workflows. Cowork is not a CLI and does not interact with your filesystem. Instead, it focuses on:

  • Collaborative document creation rather than code generation
  • Team-wide knowledge sharing rather than individual codebase understanding
  • Business workflows (proposals, reports, research) rather than engineering tasks
  • Governance and compliance rather than development tooling
💡

The key value proposition: One AI assistant that knows your team's context, standards, and goals -- ensuring consistent, high-quality output across every person in your organization.

Common Use Cases

Team Document Creation

Multiple team members collaborating on proposals, reports, and presentations with consistent brand voice and messaging.

Collaborative Research

Teams pooling insights through shared conversations, building on each other's findings with persistent context about the research domain.

Standardized Processes

Ensuring every team member follows the same procedures -- from customer onboarding scripts to compliance checklists -- with shared prompt templates.

Cross-Functional Alignment

Breaking down silos by giving different departments (marketing, legal, sales) a shared AI workspace that understands the full business context.

2. Setting Up Your Workspace

Getting started with Cowork involves creating a workspace, inviting your team, and configuring the shared context that makes Cowork powerful. A well-configured workspace is the foundation for team-wide AI adoption.

Creating a Workspace

A workspace is your team's home base in Cowork. Choose a name that reflects its purpose -- this could be your department ("Marketing Team"), a project ("Q2 Product Launch"), or your entire organization. You can create multiple workspaces for different purposes.

Roles and Permissions

Cowork uses a role-based access model to control what each team member can do. Understanding these roles is essential for proper workspace governance.

Owner

Full control over the workspace. Can delete the workspace, transfer ownership, and manage billing. Typically the person who created the workspace or a designated administrator.

Admin

Can manage team members, modify workspace settings, upload shared context, set usage policies, and view audit logs. Cannot delete the workspace or transfer ownership.

Member

Can use Claude within the workspace, create and participate in conversations, and access shared context. Cannot modify workspace settings or manage other users.

Viewer

Read-only access. Can view conversations and shared resources but cannot create new conversations or interact with Claude. Useful for stakeholders who need visibility.

Setting Shared Context

Shared context is what makes Cowork fundamentally more powerful than individual Claude sessions. By uploading your organization's documents, you give Claude persistent knowledge that applies to every conversation in the workspace.

What to upload:

  • Brand guidelines: Tone of voice, writing style, visual identity standards
  • Product documentation: Feature descriptions, pricing, technical specifications
  • Process documents: SOPs, checklists, approval workflows
  • Company policies: HR policies, compliance requirements, security standards
  • Templates: Email templates, report formats, proposal structures
ⓘ

Best practice: Start small. Upload your most important 3-5 documents first, test conversations against them, then gradually expand. Overwhelming the context with too many documents at once can reduce relevance.

Custom Instructions

Workspace-level custom instructions tell Claude how to behave for every conversation in the workspace. These are different from individual Claude.ai instructions -- they apply to the entire team.

Example workspace instructions You are an AI assistant for Acme Corp's marketing team. Always follow our brand voice: - Professional but approachable - Data-driven claims with sources - Avoid superlatives (no "best", "fastest", "revolutionary") - Use active voice When creating content: - Reference our brand guidelines document - Follow our content calendar priorities - Include relevant metrics where available - Flag any claims that need fact-checking For compliance: - Never make promises about future features - Always include required disclaimers - Follow FTC guidelines for sponsored content

Organizing by Department or Project

Most organizations benefit from creating separate workspaces for different purposes:

  • Department workspaces: Marketing, Sales, Legal, HR -- each with their own context and instructions
  • Project workspaces: Product launches, client engagements, research initiatives -- scoped to specific goals
  • Cross-functional workspaces: For initiatives that span multiple teams, combining context from each
⚠

Avoid workspace sprawl. Creating too many workspaces makes it hard for team members to find the right one. Start with one per major team or initiative, and split only when the shared context would meaningfully differ.

3. Collaborative Workflows

The real power of Cowork emerges when teams use it together. Here are the key collaboration patterns that organizations are using effectively.

Shared Conversations

Unlike individual Claude sessions, Cowork conversations can include multiple team members. Everyone sees the same thread, can add to the conversation, and benefits from the accumulated context.

When to use shared conversations:

  • Collaborative brainstorming sessions where multiple perspectives are needed
  • Document drafting where different team members own different sections
  • Decision-making processes where everyone needs to see the reasoning
  • Training scenarios where a senior team member demonstrates effective prompting

Handoff Patterns

One of the most powerful collaboration patterns is the handoff -- starting a task with Claude, building up context, and then passing the conversation to a colleague who continues the work.

The critical benefit of handoffs is that Claude retains all the context from the previous contributor. Bob does not need Alice to write a summary or re-explain the background -- Claude already understands the full conversation history.

Review Cycles

Teams can use Claude as a collaborative reviewer in their workflows:

  • First draft + AI review: A team member writes a draft, then asks Claude to review it against the workspace's guidelines and standards.
  • Peer review augmentation: Before sending a document to a human reviewer, have Claude flag issues to catch the easy ones first.
  • Consistency checking: Ask Claude to compare a new document against previously approved examples in the shared context to ensure consistency.

Template Sharing

Prompt templates are reusable starting points that encode your team's best practices into structured prompts. Instead of every team member figuring out how to ask Claude for help from scratch, templates provide a consistent, optimized starting point.

Examples of useful templates:

  • Customer email response: A template that includes the customer's issue, your product context, and tone guidelines
  • Meeting summary: A template that structures meeting notes into action items, decisions, and follow-ups
  • Competitive analysis: A template that guides Claude through a structured comparison framework
  • Job description writer: A template that ensures all job posts follow company format and diversity language
💡

Template tip: The best templates are not just prompts -- they include examples of good output. Show Claude what a great customer email or meeting summary looks like, and it will consistently produce similar quality.

4. Governance and Enterprise Controls

For organizations adopting AI at scale, governance is not optional -- it is a requirement. Cowork provides the controls that enterprise IT, legal, and compliance teams need to feel confident about team-wide AI adoption.

Usage Policies

Workspace administrators can define clear policies about what employees can and cannot use Claude for. These policies are visible to all members and help prevent misuse.

  • Approved use cases: Content creation, research summarization, process documentation
  • Restricted use cases: Sharing personally identifiable information (PII), making legal commitments, generating financial advice
  • Prohibited use cases: Sharing trade secrets, bypassing compliance processes, creating misleading content

Content Filtering and Moderation

Cowork includes configurable content filtering that administrators can tune to match their organization's needs. This ensures Claude's responses align with company standards even when users make ambiguous or inappropriate requests.

Audit Logs

Enterprise audit logging tracks who used Claude, what they asked about, and when the interaction occurred. This is critical for:

  • Compliance evidence: Demonstrating to regulators that AI usage follows approved policies
  • Usage analysis: Understanding how teams use Claude to optimize training and templates
  • Incident response: If an issue arises, audit logs provide the forensic trail needed for investigation
  • Cost accountability: Tracking usage by team, project, or individual for budget planning
⚠

Critical for regulated industries: If your organization operates under HIPAA, SOC 2, GDPR, or similar frameworks, audit logs are not just a nice-to-have -- they are a compliance requirement. Work with your compliance team to configure logging before rolling out Cowork.

Cost Management

Cowork provides tools to keep AI spending predictable and accountable:

  • Budgets: Set monthly spending limits per workspace or per team
  • Quotas: Limit the number of messages per user to prevent runaway usage
  • Alerts: Get notified when spending approaches budget thresholds (e.g., 75% and 90%)
  • Usage dashboards: Visual breakdowns of spending by team, project, and use case

Compliance Considerations

Different regulatory frameworks have different requirements for AI usage:

Framework Key Requirement How Cowork Helps
HIPAA Protected health information (PHI) safeguards Data handling policies, access controls, audit trails
SOC 2 Security, availability, and confidentiality controls Role-based access, encryption, monitoring
GDPR Data subject rights, processing transparency Usage policies, data retention controls, audit logs
FedRAMP US government cloud security standards SSO integration, centralized identity management

SSO Integration and Identity Management

Enterprise deployments integrate with existing identity providers through SSO (Single Sign-On). This means team members use their existing corporate credentials to access Cowork -- no separate accounts to manage, and automatic deprovisioning when someone leaves the organization.

ⓘ

Note: SSO integration supports SAML 2.0 and OIDC providers including Okta, Azure AD, Google Workspace, and OneLogin. Work with your IT team to configure SSO before inviting team members.

5. When to Use What -- A Decision Framework

With Claude available through the browser, the CLI (Claude Code), and Cowork, choosing the right surface for your task is important. Here is a practical guide.

Need Browser Code Cowork
Individual writing/research Best -- Good
Team document creation -- -- Best
Software development -- Best --
Enterprise governance -- -- Best
Quick personal tasks Best Good --
Shared prompt libraries -- -- Best
Code review & debugging -- Best --
Client-facing deliverables Good -- Best

The Simple Decision Rule

When choosing which Claude surface to use, ask yourself three questions:

  1. Am I writing code? If yes, use Claude Code.
  2. Does this involve my team, shared context, or governance? If yes, use Cowork.
  3. Is this a quick personal task? If yes, use the Browser.
💡

Remember: The surfaces are complementary, not competing. A software team might use Claude Code for development, Cowork for documentation and team processes, and the browser for individual research. The goal is to use the right tool for each task.
